The Work in Practice

What becomes possible

"I haven't thought about — or craved — sugar for several days now. How weird is that?"

Anita came to me while her husband was in the final stages of a chronic illness. A mother, a professional who spent her days supporting others — she was also her husband's primary caretaker, managing everything, holding everything together. She had started overeating as a way to cope, which is so common and so human, and she didn't know how to stop.

We began where the body always begins — with what's present. But as we worked, something older came into the room. Her relationship with her mother. A childhood where nurturing had been scarce. A deep, unmet hunger that had nothing to do with food.

It isn't easy to give care when we never learned what it felt like to receive it. Her body had found its own way to fill that gap — and we were able to gently, without force, bring healing to that original wound. We worked with the archetypal mother — the mother energy she had always needed — and offered her nervous system something it had been waiting a long time for.

A few days after our sessions, Anita sent me a note: "I haven't thought about — or craved — sugar for several days now. How weird is that?"

Weeks later, she told me she had placed one small candy in a bowl on her counter. Not to eat — to remind herself that she no longer needed it.

That is what healing looks like when it goes all the way down.

"That sounds like forgiveness."

Elena came to me months after an injury that had left her system shaken. She had tension in her body, migraines, some memory issues, and a low mood that had been present even before the injury.

We started there — moving the qi and blood stagnation, according to the TCM diagnosis. After just one session, her migraines eased significantly. Her neck and shoulders felt more relaxed. She reported a "really restful night's sleep."

A couple of weeks later, she naturally brought up something she hadn't shared before. Years ago, during a medical procedure, there had been a moment where she didn't feel safe or fully seen. That experience had stayed with her in her body long after the event.

In session, it surfaced while we were working near an area that still held tension and memory. We paused the bodywork and stayed with what was present.

I explained that I sometimes work with a process that supports the release of energetic imprints that can remain in the system after difficult experiences. These imprints can linger in the body. And they can take up space.

She paused and said:

"That sounds like forgiveness."

I smiled.

"Sometimes it is. But it doesn't require forgiveness. It simply asks: Do you want to keep carrying this in your body? If not, we can let it go."

We deserve to love ourselves enough to release what hurts us. So I asked her gently: "Do you give permission to release the energy connected to that experience?"

She said, "Absolutely."

"That energy does not belong to you. We're going to let it go."

She nodded — clear and steady.

Every release begins with permission. I always ask clearly, and they respond clearly. Only then does the work begin.

Later that week she told me the session had been profound. That she could still feel the effervescent, sparkly energy that had helped clear what had been held there for so long.

In the weeks that followed, her creativity began returning.

And a month later — the scar that had always brought the memory back with it was fading.

For the People Who Tend to Everyone Else

I work with healers

"If you spend your days holding space for other people's healing — this is for you."

Not as a specialty I chose, but as a pattern I noticed. When I look back at the people I've done my deepest work with — the ones whose stories stay with me — they are almost always people who spend their lives in service to others.

The Waldorf school teacher. The healer who carried sixty years of nightmares she had never told anyone about. The special education worker whose body had absorbed years of others' stress. The professional who gave everything to everyone around her, while her own needs quietly went unmet.

Therapists Bodyworkers Acupuncturists Massage Therapists Energy Healers Teachers Social Workers Nurses & Caregivers Movement Practitioners Activists Mothers Executives & Leaders

The executive who holds the room together all day and has nowhere to put what that costs.

Healers are often the last to receive healing. You know the language. You understand the body. You believe in this work. And yet — precisely because you give so much — your own system accumulates what your clients release. You carry what others couldn't.

You don't need me to explain what qi is. You don't need convincing that the body holds memory. What you may need is someone who can reach the places you can't reach in yourself — because we cannot always do our own deepest work alone.

You deserve the same quality of care you offer everyone else. Let someone tend to you for a change.

About Gwen

Origin

I came to this work because it's what I needed for myself.

This world is overwhelming. It can shake you to your core. There is so much to feel, so much to absorb, so much to become — and nobody warns you how much of what you're carrying might not even be yours.

I felt a great deal from the time I was young. The world came in loud and I didn't always know where to put it. I lived for years with anxiety, with depression, with a feeling of impending doom that followed me like a shadow. I didn't have a name for what I was. I just knew something was wrong — or that something was a lot.

What I didn't know yet was that I came from a long line of people who felt deeply and had no container for it. The fracture went back generations. Unmet need passing quietly from one person to the next, each one doing their best with what they had, none of them knowing they were extraordinary.

I am a wilder. I inherited the gifts, not the tradition. I have spent my life finding the name of what I already was — and learning how to work with what I carry. The language I speak now took years to build. It is entirely my own.

What I found changed everything.

And now I work with people who know what it feels like to carry too much — people who feel deeply, give generously, and are ready to finally put down what was never theirs to hold.

The people who shaped me were not perfect. Neither were the relationships. But what they passed down — often without knowing — was real and worth keeping.

My father's mother was Irish and magical in ways the family whispered about but never quite named. She had a particular relationship with grace — knowing when it would come, never asking for more than she needed. That restraint was its own kind of wisdom, and it passed down through the line.

My father never called himself a healer. But he grew a garden every year of my childhood, turning the soil by hand, tending what he planted with steady daily care. When I had a splinter, he heated a needle over a flame and coaxed it out with a precision and tenderness I now recognize as clinical — the same quality of attention I bring to my work with bodies. He taught me that hands are instruments of care. He just didn't call it that.

My mother was a sensitive. She had no language for what she felt and not enough confidence to trust it. I recognized her gifts long after I had found words for my own. I carry her too.

My brother placed books in my hands that became my foundation — herbalism, homeopathy, Chinese medicine. When I was lost in my early twenties, he wrote me a letter about how to find my way back through food and plants. I still have that letter.

None of them knew they were extraordinary. I know now. And knowing is its own kind of calling.
