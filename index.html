The Work in Practice

What becomes possible

"The scar from the surgery — the one that had been bothersome for years — was almost completely gone."

She came to me a couple of months earlier — months after a fall where she'd hit her head and never gone to the doctor. She had tension in her body, migraines, memory loss, depression that had been with her even before the fall.

We started with the fall. Moving the qi and blood stagnation — the TCM diagnosis. After just one session, her migraines had stopped. Her neck and shoulders were more relaxed. She reported a "really solid night's sleep."

A couple of weeks later, she told me something she hadn't mentioned before. Years ago, while she was being prepped for surgery, the nurses got in an argument in front of her. One of them smacked tape down hard on her body — she thinks they thought she was already under anesthesia. That memory had tormented her ever since.

We were working near the scar when she told me this. I transitioned into Theta Healing and asked her if she wanted to release the trauma from her body.

She said: "Heck yeah."

During the work, she felt energy leaving her body. Then she felt something else leave — a male energy. Her first husband, who had left her suddenly with no explanation many years ago. She felt his energy leaving too.

Later that week she told me the session was profound. That she still felt the effervescent, sparkly energy that had helped clear what had been held there for so long.

A month and a half later: the scar from the surgery, which had been bothersome to her when she first mentioned it, had softened so much that it was almost completely gone. Her body had finally been given permission to finish healing. Another beutiful thing that happened is her creativity started coming back and she was diving into a new project that filled her with excitement.

About Gwen Joseph

I came to this work sideways

Not through a clean lineage with named teachers and a clear path handed down — but through noticing what was already in my bones.

My father never called himself a healer. But he grew a garden every year of my childhood, turning the soil by hand, tending what he planted with steady daily care. When I had a splinter, he heated a needle over a flame and coaxed it out with a precision and tenderness I now recognize as clinical — the same quality of attention I bring to my work with bodies. He taught me that hands are instruments of care. He just didn't call it that.

My brother placed books in my hands that became my foundation — herbalism, homeopathy, Chinese medicine. When I was lost in my early twenties, he wrote me a letter about how to find my way back through food and plants. I still have that letter.

I am the one who finally gets to speak what was passed down without ceremony — because the people passing it didn't know they were extraordinary.

I know now. And knowing is its own kind of calling.
