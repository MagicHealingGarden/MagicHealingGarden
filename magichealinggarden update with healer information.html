The Work in Practice

What becomes possible

"I haven't thought about — or craved — sugar for several days now. How weird is that?"

Anita came to me while her husband was in the final stages of a chronic illness. A mother, a martial artist, a professional who spent her days supporting others — she was also her husband's primary caretaker, managing everything, holding everything together. She had started overeating as a way to cope, which is so common and so human, and she didn't know how to stop.

We began where the body always begins — with what's present. But as we worked, something older came into the room. Her relationship with her mother. A childhood where nurturing had been scarce. A deep, unmet hunger that had nothing to do with food.

It isn't easy to give care when we never learned what it felt like to receive it. Her body had found its own way to fill that gap — and we were able to gently, without force, bring healing to that original wound. We worked with the archetypal mother — the mother energy she had always needed — and offered her nervous system something it had been waiting a long time for.

A few days after our sessions, Anita sent me a note.

"I haven't thought about — or craved — sugar for several days now. How weird is that?"

Weeks later, she told me she had placed one small candy in a bowl on her counter. Not to eat — to remind herself that she no longer needed it.

That is what healing looks like when it goes all the way down.

Who Finds Me

For the people who tend to everyone else

"If you spend your days holding space for other people's healing — this is for you."

I work with healers. Not as a specialty I chose, but as a pattern I noticed. When I look back at the people I've done my deepest work with — the ones whose stories stay with me — they are almost always people who spend their lives in service to others.

The Waldorf school teacher who carried sixty years of nightmares she had never told anyone about. The special education worker whose body had absorbed years of others' stress. The professional in continuing medical education — and a mother and martial artist — who gave everything to everyone around her, while her own needs quietly went unmet.

Healers are often the last to receive healing. You know the language. You understand the body. You believe in this work. And yet — precisely because you give so much — your own system accumulates what your clients release. You carry what others couldn't.

Therapists Bodyworkers Acupuncturists Massage Therapists Energy Healers Teachers Social Workers Nurses & Caregivers Movement Practitioners Activists Mothers

You don't need me to explain what qi is. You don't need convincing that the body holds memory. What you may need is someone who can reach the places you can't reach in yourself — because we cannot always do our own deepest work alone.

"You deserve the same quality of care you offer everyone else. Let someone tend to you for a change."

About Gwen Joseph

I came to this work sideways

Not through a clean lineage with named teachers and a clear path handed down — but through noticing what was already in my bones.

My father never called himself a healer. But he grew a garden every year of my childhood, turning the soil by hand, tending what he planted with steady daily care. When I had a splinter, he heated a needle over a flame and coaxed it out with a precision and tenderness I now recognize as clinical — the same quality of attention I bring to my work with bodies. He taught me that hands are instruments of care. He just didn't call it that.

My brother placed books in my hands that became my foundation — herbalism, homeopathy, Chinese medicine. When I was lost in my early twenties, he wrote me a letter about how to find my way back through food and plants. I still have that letter.

I am the one who finally gets to speak what was passed down without ceremony — because the people passing it didn't know they were extraordinary.

I know now. And knowing is its own kind of calling.
